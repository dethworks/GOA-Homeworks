Mount Machu Pichu



Machu Picchu is an ancient Inca citadel located in the Andes Mountains of Peru, near the city of Cusco. Often called the "Lost City of the Incas," it is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and one of the New Seven Wonders of the World, renowned for its sophisticated architecture and breathtaking location.

Machu Picchu sits at an elevation of approximately 2,430 meters (7,970 feet) on a mountain ridge. It overlooks the Urubamba River valley, surrounded by lush, steep slopes and misty cloud forests, making it an exceptional example of human adaptation to the natural environment. The sites location helped it remain hidden from the Spanish conquistadors, preserving its unique features for centuries.

Built in the mid-15th century under the Inca emperor Pachacuti, Machu Picchu served multiple purposes, including a royal estate, religious site, and a center for agricultural and astronomical studies. The site was abandoned around the time of the Spanish conquest in the 16th century, remaining largely unknown to the outside world until it was brought to international attention by American explorer Hiram Bingham in 1911.



The architecture of Machu Picchu is notable for its dry-stone construction method, with stones cut so precisely that no mortar was needed to hold them together. Key structures include the Temple of the Sun, Intihuatana (a ritual stone associated with astronomical observations), the Temple of the Three Windows, and the main residential and agricultural areas. These structures are laid out to align with celestial events, reflecting the Incas sophisticated knowledge of astronomy.

Machu Picchus layout is divided into the agricultural terraces on the outskirts, used to grow crops, and the urban area, containing temples, plazas, and residential areas. The terraces also helped stabilize the mountain slope and manage water drainage.

Today, Machu Picchu attracts millions of visitors each year, leading to significant conservation challenges. Efforts are ongoing to protect the site from erosion, environmental degradation, and overcrowding, balancing tourism with preservation to maintain its cultural and historical value for future generations.
